MAE 4272: Aerodynamic Optimization of a 3D-Printed Wind Turbine Blade

Project Overview

I was challenged to design a high-efficiency, 6-inch radius wind turbine blade optimized for a real-world wind environment. The primary objective was to maximize the Expected Power Output based on a specific Weibull probability distribution of wind speeds.

This project required balancing aerodynamic performance with manufacturing constraints, such as 3D-printability and structural stability up to 3000 RPM.

Figure 1: Final 3D CAD model featuring optimized chord and twist distribution.

Design Process & Optimization

The team utilized Blade Element Momentum (BEM) Theory to model the interaction between the blade geometry and the incoming airflow. We selected the NACA 4412 airfoil profile for its high lift-to-drag ratio at low Reynolds numbers.

Figure 2: Theoretical distribution of twist and chord length across the span.

Key design decisions were made through an iterative script that calculated local induction factors to solve for the optimal twist angle and chord length at 10 discrete stations along the blade. We prioritized a robust Factor of Safety (FOS=2) to ensure the blade could withstand peak aerodynamic loads during high-speed operation.

Testing & Performance Validation

The physical blades were fabricated using 3D Systems Accura 25 resin and tested in a subsonic wind tunnel. We established a linear calibration between fan frequency and wind velocity before generating experimental power curves. By sweeping through torque brake voltages at multiple wind speeds, we identified the peak power points for our design.

Data analysis involved integrating the experimental power curves with the environmental Weibull distribution. This allowed us to determine that our blade achieves an expected power of 0.087 W with an optimal operating speed of 850 RPM.

Figure 3: Experimental Power vs. RPM curves used to find the optimal operating point.
