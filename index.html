Optimized Wind Turbine Blade Design

Executive Summary

This project details the design, fabrication, and testing of an optimized 6-inch wind turbine blade designed for maximum power efficiency. Using Blade Element Momentum (BEM) theory and a NACA 4412 airfoil profile, our team developed a blade geometry optimized for a specific Weibull-distributed wind environment. The final design was successfully 3D-printed and tested in a wind tunnel to validate its performance against theoretical models.

Key Result: The design achieved a maximum expected power output (Pexp) of 0.087 W at an optimal rotation rate (Ωopt) of 850 RPM.

Objectives & Constraints

The primary objective was to develop a 6-inch radius blade specifically designed to maximize expected power output within a Weibull-distributed wind environment. This required selecting an appropriate airfoil and designing the optimal twist and chord distributions to maintain efficient aerodynamic loading across the span.

The design was governed by several rigorous constraints:

  • Geometric: Radius ≤ 6 in, Thickness < 2 in, and Chord < 1.6 in.
  • Manufacturing: 3D printable, no thin trailing edges, and a seamless hub attachment.
  • Operational: Stable operation up to 3000 RPM with a Factor of Safety (FOS) of 2 under aerodynamic loads.

Design Process & Methodology

We followed a 6-step iterative design process centered on BEM theory:

  1. Parameter Definition: Established the baseline radius and environmental constants.
  2. Airfoil Selection: Chose the NACA 4412 for its high CL/CD ratio.
  3. Theoretical Modeling: Determined maximum theoretical power production.
  4. Geometric Iteration: Refined chord, pitch, twist, and thickness across the blade span.
  5. Operational Analysis: Simulated increased rotation rates until physical constraints were reached.
  6. Validation: Finalizing the design for manufacturing.

Experimental Testing & Validation

Testing was conducted in two main phases. First, we established a linear relationship between the wind tunnel fan frequency (Hz) and actual wind velocity (m/s). Second, we generated Power Curves by recording turbine RPM and power output across multiple wind speeds.

By weighting these power curves against the Weibull Probability Distribution, we were able to identify the "expected power" across all probable wind speeds in the target environment.

Conclusion

The project successfully validated that BEM theory can accurately guide the design of small-scale turbine blades. The manufactured design met all structural and geometric requirements while providing a clear path toward optimal efficiency. Future iterations could improve model accuracy by collecting more data in the 3-7 m/s wind range and refining the hub attachment to reduce mechanical friction.
