MAE 4272: Optimization of a 3D-Printed Wind Turbine Blade

Project Overview

I collaborated with a team to design and optimize a 6-inch radius wind turbine blade. The goal was to maximize expected power output within a Weibull-distributed wind environment.

This required navigating complex constraints, including 3D-printability, a maximum chord of 1.6 inches, and ensuring structural integrity up to 3000 RPM with a factor of safety of 2.

Figure 1: Final CAD geometry optimized for aerodynamic loading and manufacturability.

Aerodynamic Modeling

Figure 2: Theoretical Power vs. Rotation Rate derived from BEM modeling.

We utilized Blade Element Momentum (BEM) Theory to iteratively solve for the optimal twist and chord distribution. By modeling the NACA 4412 airfoil across ten discrete radial stations, we predicted the turbine's performance limits.

This modeling phase allowed us to calculate the maximum theoretical power before proceeding to fabrication, providing a baseline for our experimental trials.

Testing & Experimental Summary

The blade was tested in a subsonic wind tunnel using a torque brake system. We generated experimental power curves across a range of wind velocities from 4.5 m/s to 10.6 m/s.

By applying the Weibull probability distribution to our experimental data, we calculated an expected power of 0.087 W at an optimal rotation rate of 850 RPM.

Figure 3: Experimental power curves showing the peak operating points across varying wind speeds.

My Contribution

In this group project, I was responsible for:

  • Data Analysis: Developed the script to integrate experimental power curves with the Weibull distribution to determine the final optimal operating point.
  • Experimental Setup: Managed the wind tunnel fan frequency calibration and torque brake voltage adjustments during data collection.
  • Technical Reporting: Drafted the structural analysis sections and performance comparison against theoretical BEM predictions.
